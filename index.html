Chicago's Carbide & Carbon Building

At the height of Prohibition in 1929, the Carbide & Carbon Building was completed in its stunning Art Deco style on Chicago's famous Michigan Avenue. Designed by the Burnham Brothers, the building was intentionally modeled after a dark green Champagne bottle wrapped in gold foil—a symbol of prosperity, celebration, and optimism during one of the nation's most restrictive eras. At the time, Chicago Mayor William "Big Bill" Thompson openly opposed federal Prohibition enforcement, campaigning on promises to keep Chicago a "wet" city despite the nationwide ban on alcohol. Local legend even suggests that the building was designed to proclaim Chicago as the "wettest" city in the United States, though historians generally regard this story as folklore rather than documented fact.
